Classical elastoplastic models are constrained by a priori mathematical assumptions, purely data-driven approaches lack thermodynamic admissibility and interpretability, and existing physics-informed neural networks focus on solving known equations rather than discovering unknown constitutive laws. This research proposes a physics-driven deep learning framework, positioned within the AI for Science paradigm, in which thermodynamic first principles are embedded as hard architectural constraints to enable constitutive discovery. Four interrelated research objectives are pursued: (RO1) developing a thermodynamically consistent neural constitutive network (t-PiNet) with exact second-law enforcement; (RO2) extracting interpretable constitutive laws via symbolic regression; (RO3) validating the framework under cyclic loading using Norwegian Geotechnical Institute experimental data; and (RO4) extending to the grain scale through a physics-consistent graph neural network surrogate for discrete element simulation. Preliminary results demonstrate accurate stress–strain predictions across multiple over-consolidation ratios, guaranteed thermodynamic admissibility, and generalization to unseen confining pressures without retraining, providing evidence that hard physical constraints serve as effective inductive biases for out-of-distribution generalization.Key words: constitutive modeling, geomaterials, AI for Science, thermodynamic hard constraints, symbolic regression, scientific discovery.ContentsAbstract	I1. mechanical behavior of geomaterials constitutes the fundamental scientific basis for the long-term safety and serviceability of geotechnical foundations, offshore energy infrastructures, and transportation systems. As representative granular materials, geomaterials exhibit highly complex mechanical responses. Their constitutive behavior is strongly influenced by stress path and loading history (path dependency), density state and effective confining pressure (state dependency), microstructural features and cementation (structural effects), as well as accumulated deformation and pore pressure evolution under cyclic loading such as waves and earthquakes (cyclic degradation). These characteristics render the formulation of a unified and comprehensive constitutive equation capable of covering all engineering scenarios exceedingly challenging. Consequently, constitutive modeling of geomaterials has long been regarded as one of the central challenges in geomechanics.Traditionally, this problem has been addressed through the development of phenomenological constitutive models. Since the 1960s, a series of landmark models have been proposed within the framework of classical elastoplasticity, including the Modified Cam-Clay model (Roscoe and Burland, 1968), boundary surface plasticity models (Dafalias, 1986; Dafalias and Herrmann, 1986), and state-parameter-based constitutive models (Been and Jefferies, 1985; Li and Dafalias, 2000). These models are theoretically rigorous, physically interpretable, and extensively validated in engineering practice. Nevertheless, they fundamentally rely on a number of empirical assumptions, such as the a priori selection of yield surface geometry, prescribed functional forms of hardening laws, and the adoption of associated or non-associated flow rules. Once the mathematical structure is specified, predictive accuracy may deteriorate under unconventional stress paths, cross-scale material variability, or extreme dynamic loading conditions. Furthermore, increasing model complexity, especially through the introduction of multiple bounding surfaces and internal variables to capture cyclic behavior, leads to a substantial growth in the number of parameters that need to be calibrated. Some of these parameters lack clear physical interpretation, making calibration labor-intensive and heavily dependent on engineering judgment.In recent years, rapid advances in machine learning and deep learning have introduced a new data-driven paradigm for constitutive modeling. Artificial neural networks (ANNs), recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks have been successfully employed to fit stress–strain responses of geomaterials under specific loading conditions. Compared with traditional models, data-driven approaches do not require predefined functional forms and, in principle, possess the capacity to approximate arbitrary nonlinear mappings. However, purely data-driven methods suffer from fundamental limitations. First, their extrapolation capability is often limited; predictive reliability deteriorates sharply outside the range of the training data. Second, they may violate fundamental physical principles. Neural-network-based constitutive models do not inherently guarantee energy consistency along stress paths, nor do they ensure non-negative dissipation, potentially violating thermodynamic laws and introducing systematic errors.The advent of physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019) seeks to mitigate these issues by embedding governing physical equations into the loss function of neural networks as soft constraints. PINNs have achieved notable success in solving problems in fluid mechanics, heat transfer, and elasticity, and have subsequently been introduced into geomechanics for applications such as consolidation analysis and boundary value problems. However, the prevailing paradigm of PINN research in geotechnical engineering exhibits a critical methodological limitation. In most existing studies, PINNs are employed primarily as numerical solvers for partial differential equations, effectively replacing conventional numerical methods such as the finite element method. This represents an upgrade at the computational tool level rather than a breakthrough in the conceptual framework of constitutive modeling. If the constitutive equation itself remains unknown or incomplete, then solving existing governing equations using PINNs offers limited contribution toward establishing more accurate constitutive descriptions./Fig. 1 Overview of the development of PINNsIn light of the above analysis, this study aims to explore a novel methodological pathway. Under the constraint of thermodynamic first principles, we seek to automatically discover interpretable constitutive laws directly from data. Furthermore, this framework is extended to discrete particulate systems by investigating graph neural network–based simulation approaches constrained by fundamental physical laws such as momentum conservation. Through this integrated strategy, the study aspires to provide a new tool for geomaterial constitutive modeling that simultaneously ensures physical admissibility, interpretability, and predictive capability.Research problem statementDespite the promise of physics-informed machine learning for constitutive modelling, several fundamental challenges remain unresolved. This thesis addresses four research questions. Each question is independently answerable and corresponds to a planned journal publication. They form a coherent progression from theoretical foundation (RQ1), through knowledge extraction (RQ2), to experimental validation (RQ3), and grain-scale extension (RQ4).RQ1 (Paper 1): Can we embed thermodynamic laws as hard constraints in neural constitutive models?Existing PINN frameworks impose physical laws as soft penalty terms in the loss function. As a result, thermodynamic admissibility is only approximately satisfied and sensitive to hyper-parameter tuning. For geomaterials, the first law (energy conservation) and second law (non-negative dissipation) of thermodynamics are more fundamental than any particular constitutive equation. Can we design a neural network architecture that satisfies both laws as exact hard constraints, so that every prediction is guaranteed to be thermodynamically admissible by construction? Specifically, the architecture would use automatic differentiation to enforce stress-energy consistency and constrained activation functions to guarantee non-negative dissipation.RQ2 (Paper 2): To what extent can symbolic regression recover interpretable constitutive laws from trained neural networks?Even a thermodynamically admissible neural network encodes its knowledge implicitly in weights and biases. This makes the model difficult to validate against theory, hard to implement in standard finite element codes, and unable to advance scientific understanding the way an explicit equation does. To what extent can symbolic regression techniques recover closed-form constitutive expressions from a trained neural network? How do the discovered expressions compare with established analytical models in accuracy and simplicity? The target expressions include Helmholtz free energy functions, yield surfaces, and hardening laws.RQ3 (Paper 3): How can a physics-constrained neural constitutive model be extended from monotonic synthetic data to real cyclic data?The model developed in RQ1 is initially trained on monotonic synthetic data from the Modified Cam-Clay model. This training data cannot capture cyclic phenomena such as hysteresis, accumulated plastic strain, and excess pore-pressure buildup. These effects are essential for offshore foundation design. Moreover, real laboratory data contain measurement noise and material-specific behaviours absent from simulations. How can a thermodynamically constrained neural constitutive model be systematically extended to cyclic loading and adapted to real experimental data from marine clay tests? Can thermodynamic admissibility be preserved throughout the adaptation process?RQ4 (Paper 4): Can a graph neural network learn inter-particle contact laws while respecting Newtonian mechanics?The first three questions operate at the continuum scale. However, many geomaterial phenomena, such as strain localisation, fabric anisotropy, and particle crushing, originate at the grain scale and cannot be fully captured by continuum assumptions. Discrete Element Method (DEM) simulations can resolve grain-scale mechanics but are computationally expensive and rely on empirically calibrated contact laws. Can we build a differentiable graph neural network simulator for granular materials that learns inter-particle contact laws from DEM or experimental data while embedding Newton's third law and momentum conservation as inductive biases? To what extent does this approach improve over hand-crafted DEM contact models?Together, these four research questions define the scope of this dissertation. The framework guarantees thermodynamic admissibility (RQ1), discovers interpretable constitutive laws (RQ2), extends to real cyclic loading via sim-to-real adaptation (RQ3), and generalises to discrete particulate systems (RQ4). Each question yields an independent journal publication, yet the answers build on one another to form a unified contribution to geotechnical constitutive modelling.Research aims and objectivesTo address the aforementioned central research question, this project establishes four specific and interrelated research objectives:Research Objective 1: Develop a thermodynamically consistent constitutive neural network framework with hard constraintsThis objective aims to construct a thermodynamically consistent neural network architecture centered on the Helmholtz free energy potential and a dissipation potential. In this framework, stresses are derived explicitly through automatic differentiation of the free energy with respect to strain, thereby guaranteeing energetic consistency by construction. Moreover, the non-negativity of dissipation is enforced at the architectural level rather than imposed as a soft penalty term.A key component of this objective is the design of appropriate optimization strategies and loss formulations that enable accurate fitting of geomaterial mechanical responses under both monotonic and cyclic loading paths. The resulting framework will provide a physically admissible foundation upon which subsequent knowledge discovery can be conducted.Research Objective 2: Achieve symbolic discovery and explicit representation of geomaterial constitutive lawsBuilding upon the trained thermodynamically consistent networks developed in Objective 1, this objective seeks to extract explicit and interpretable mathematical expressions governing material behavior. Sparse regression and symbolic regression techniques will be introduced to automatically identify closed-form representations of key constitutive components, including: (1) The Helmholtz free energy function, (2) Yield surface equations, and (3) Hardening laws. Validation will be conducted using benchmark datasets, including synthetic data generated from the Modified Cam-Clay model, laboratory triaxial test data, and direct simple shear (DSS) test results. The discovered expressions will be evaluated in terms of predictive accuracy, parsimony, and physical interpretability. This objective aims to demonstrate a systematic transition from “black-box” data fitting to “white-box” knowledge discovery.Research Objective 3: Validate predictive capability under complex dynamic loading conditionsThis objective evaluates the robustness and generalization capability of the proposed framework under complex dynamic loading scenarios. Particular emphasis will be placed on cyclic shear tests involving different combinations of mean shear stress and cyclic shear amplitude.The model’s ability to reproduce key phenomena, including hysteresis loop evolution, accumulated strain, pore pressure buildup, and cyclic degradation, will be systematically evaluated. This analysis will enable a rigorous assessment of the framework’s unified predictive capability under both static and dynamic loading conditions.Research Objective 4: Explore a physics-consistent graph neural network simulator for discrete particulate systems.Extending beyond the continuum scale, this objective explores a graph neural network (GNN) architecture for discrete particulate simulation. In this formulation, particles are represented as nodes and contacts as edges, while fundamental physical principles, such as momentum conservation through action–reaction symmetry and geometric contact constraints, are incorporated into the network as inductive biases.Using classical contact mechanics models (e.g., the Hertz contact model) as baseline references, the framework will explore the network’s ability to automatically learn complex contact behaviors and particle-shape effects. The ultimate goal is to establish a differentiable, “DEM-like” surrogate simulation platform that enables efficient forward prediction and gradient-based optimization, thereby laying the groundwork for future multiscale coupling analyses.Together, these four objectives establish a coherent research roadmap, starting with thermodynamically consistent neural constitutive modeling, advancing to symbolic knowledge extraction, validating performance under complex loading, and ultimately extending the framework to discrete particle systems.Expected outcomesThis PhD project explores the intersection of computational mechanics, geotechnical engineering, and artificial intelligence, with the modest goal of contributing to "AI for Science" approaches in geomaterial constitutive modeling. This calibration complexity motivates the data-driven and knowledge discovery approaches pursued in the present research.Data-driven constitutive modelingEarly neural network approachesThe application of artificial neural networks to constitutive modeling dates to the early 1990s. Ghaboussi et al. (1991) first demonstrated that a feedforward neural network trained on triaxial test data could reproduce the stress–strain response of sand with reasonable accuracy, introducing the term "neuro-mechanical model." Ellis et al. (1995) extended this work to loading-unloading-reloading cycles, while Lefik and Schrefler (2003) incorporated neural network models as user-defined material subroutines within finite element codes.These early approaches treated the constitutive problem as a function approximation: given current strain (or strain increment) and current stress, predict the new stress (or stress increment). This static mapping formulation ignores loading history and fails catastrophically for path-dependent materials. Attempts to address this by including the loading history as additional inputs are ad hoc and do not generalize to arbitrary histories.Recurrent neural networks and LSTMThe introduction of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and, more specifically, long short-term memory (LSTM) networks (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber, 1997) provided a principled solution to the history-dependence problem. In an LSTM-based constitutive model, the network maintains a hidden state ℎ𝑡 that summarizes all information from the loading history up to time t. Thermodynamic inadmissibility: As demonstrated by Masi et al. (2021) and confirmed in the preliminary results of the present study, unconstrained neural networks frequently predict negative dissipation, violating the second law of thermodynamics. This produces unphysical energy generation that can destabilize finite element simulations.(2). Poor extrapolation: The generalization capability of data-driven models is bounded by the coverage of the training dataset. Unlike thermodynamically constrained models, whose hypothesis space is restricted to physically admissible responses, unconstrained neural networks are free to fit any mapping, including physically implausible ones, when confronted with out-of-distribution inputs.(3). Opacity: The constitutive knowledge encoded in a trained neural network cannot be examined, validated against theory, or communicated in the form of equations. This limits the scientific contribution of data-driven models and creates barriers to their adoption in engineering practice.(4). Performance overestimation: Benchmark comparisons on curated, noise-free datasets typically overstate the accuracy achievable with real experimental data, which is limited in quantity, noisier, and often not systematically collected across the full loading space.Physics-informed machine learningThe PINN frameworkPhysics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs), introduced by Raissi et al. In all the above applications, the governing PDEs are assumed to be known and are incorporated as constraints (Gao et al., 2022; Henkes et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024). However, the constitutive law describing the stress–strain relationship must be provided as an explicit input to the PINN (Haghighat et al., 2021; Tartakovsky et al., 2020; Yang and Perdikaris, 2019; Zhang et al., 2022). When the constitutive law is unknown, incomplete, or inaccurate, these deficiencies will inevitably propagate into the PINN solution, regardless of how rigorously the PDE is enforced. The present research addresses this fundamental limitation by directing the learning process toward the constitutive law itself.Limitation 2: Soft constraints do not guarantee physical admissibility. The penalty-based formulation of PINN physical constraints means that compliance with the governing equations is enforced only in an average sense over the collocation points. There is no guarantee of exact compliance at every point, particularly in regions not covered by collocation points or outside the training domain (Xiang et al., 2022). For thermodynamic constraints, which must hold everywhere and under all loading paths, this soft-constraint approach is insufficient.Thermodynamics-based neural networks Non-equilibrium thermodynamics frameworkThe theoretical foundation of the proposed framework is provided by the thermodynamics of irreversible processes, applied to elasto-plastic material behavior. While this implicit representation is sufficient for forward predictions, it is inadequate for scientific understanding, model verification, or implementation in standard engineering software. The knowledge discovery problem asks: given a trained neural network (or training data), can we automatically identify an explicit mathematical expression that captures the underlying relationship?This problem differs from classical curve fitting because the functional form is not specified in advance, and the algorithm must explore the space of possible mathematical expressions to identify one that is both accurate and efficient.Sparse regression: SINDy and variantsThe Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy) framework (Brunton et al., 2016) addresses the knowledge discovery problem by assuming that the true governing equation can be represented as a sparse linear combination of candidate basis functions from a user-defined library Θ𝐳:𝑧=Θ𝑧⋅𝚵where Ξ is a sparse coefficient matrix. The library Θ typically includes monomials up to a certain degree, trigonometric functions, exponentials, and products thereof. The sparse coefficient matrix is identified through the Sequentially Thresholded Least Squares (STLS) algorithm or L1-regularized regression (LASSO), which promotes sparsity by penalizing the L1 norm of the coefficients./Fig. 10 Schematic of the SINDy algorithm (Brunton et al., 2016)SINDy has been applied to discover governing equations for a range of physical systems, including chaotic dynamical systems, chemical reaction kinetics, and structural vibration. Messenger and Bortz (2021) extended SINDy to PDE discovery (PDEs-FIND), enabling the identification of both spatial and temporal operators from field data.For constitutive law discovery, the internal variable evolution equations ȧ = f (ε, α) and the free energy function F = g (ε, α) are natural targets for SINDy-type discovery. The key challenge lies in constructing an appropriate candidate library that incorporates physically motivated basis functions, such as logarithms of stress ratios and exponentials of state parameters, which together span the space of plausible constitutive expressions for geomaterials.Genetic programming and PySRGenetic programming (GP, Koza, 1992) is an evolutionary algorithm that represents mathematical expressions as tree structures and optimizes them through selection, crossover, and mutation operations. Unlike SINNDy, which searches within a user-defined linear function space, GP searches the full space of mathematical expressions (subject to a specified set of operators and terminal nodes), enabling the discovery of qualitatively novel functional forms.The PySR library (Cranmer, 2023) provides a modern, high-performance implementation of multi-objective symbolic regression that balances accuracy and complexity through Pareto-optimal search. PySR has been applied to discover closed-form equations in astrophysics (Cranmer et al., 2020), material science (Wang et al., 2023), and fluid mechanics, consistently recovering interpretable expressions that match or improve upon expert-derived models./Fig. 11 AI-guided generation of scientific hypotheses (Wang et al., 2023)For constitutive law discovery, GP-based symbolic regression is particularly well suited to identifying the free energy function F (ε, α), which may involve logarithmic, polynomial, and exponential terms that are difficult to include exhaustively in a SINDy library.Application to constitutive modelingThe application of symbolic regression to constitutive modeling is nascent but highly promising. Flaschel et al. (2021) applied SINDy to discover strain energy functions for hyperelastic materials from uniaxial and biaxial test data, recovering the Neo-Hookean and Mooney-Rivlin models as special cases. Abdusalamov et al. (2023) extended this to elasto-plastic materials, demonstrating the recovery of von Mises yield criteria and linear kinematic hardening laws from simulated triaxial test data./Fig. 12 Unsupervised algorithm for discovering interpretable constitutive laws (Flaschel et al., 2021)To date, no study has applied symbolic regression to thermodynamically consistent neural network models for geomaterials, representing a clear gap that RO2 of the present programme addresses.Graph neural networks for particulate system simulationDiscrete element method and its limitationsThe Discrete Element Method (DEM), pioneered by Cundall and Strack (1979), simulates granular materials by tracking the motion of individual particles and computing inter-particle contact forces according to prescribed contact laws, including normal stiffness, tangential stiffness, friction coefficient, and rolling resistance. DEM provides direct access to grain-scale phenomena such as force chain networks, fabric evolution, and particle rotation, and has become an invaluable research tool in geomechanics.However, DEM faces significant challenges in engineering practice: (1). Computational cost: Simulating even a small laboratory specimen (10⁶ particles) requires days of CPU time, making DEM unsuitable for direct use in boundary value problems. (2). Contact law prescription: The contact laws must be manually prescribed and calibrated, a non-trivial task for non-spherical particles, bonded granular materials, or particles with complex surface textures. (3). Upscaling: Extracting continuum-level constitutive relationships from DEM simulations requires careful homogenization and is sensitive to the choice of representative volume element size.Graph neural networks for particle simulationGraph neural networks (GNNs) provide a natural computational paradigm for particle systems by representing the particle assembly as a graph 𝐺=𝑉,𝐸, where vertices V represent particles and edges E represent inter-particle contacts or proximity relationships (Zhao et al., 2025). Several approaches have been developed to embed physical principles as inductive biases:(1). Lagrangian and Hamiltonian Neural Networks. Cranmer et al. (2020) and Greydanus et al. (2019) proposed parameterizing the Lagrangian 𝐿𝑞,𝑞 or Hamiltonian 𝐻𝑞,𝑝 of a system with a neural network, then using the Euler–Lagrange or Hamilton equations to derive the equations of motion. This can be embedded by requiring the contact force message to satisfy 𝑚𝑖𝑗=−𝑚𝑗𝑖, which is achieved by constructing the message function as an anti-symmetric function of the relative particle features.(4). Dynami-CAL GraphNet. A significant recent advance is the Dynami-CAL GraphNet (Sharma and Fink, 2026), which embeds both linear and angular momentum conservation simultaneously through edge-local reference frames. The key insight is to decompose each inter-particle contact force into three physically interpretable components: (a) an antisymmetric internal force vector satisfying Newton's third law exactly, (b) a pairwise angular interaction vector governing torque exchange, and (c) a force application point determining rotational dynamics. This decomposition guarantees that the system’s total linear and angular momentum are conserved exactly at each timestep by architectural design, not merely in expectation. Importantly, Dynami-CAL has been shown to extrapolate across more than 16,000 consecutive timesteps without violating physical constraints and to scale from training on dozens of particles to inference on thousands, making it highly suitable as a DEM surrogate in geomechanics.(5). Complementing particle-level physics-consistent GNNs, the NeuralDEM framework (Alkin et al., 2025) takes a macroscopic approach: rather than tracking individual particle trajectories, NeuralDEM learns mappings between macroscopic field variables (velocity fields, density fields, stress fields) using neural operators, enabling real-time simulation of industrial particulate flows at scales inaccessible to conventional DEM. This macroscopic approach is particularly relevant to geomechanics applications where continuum-level predictions (void ratio fields, fabric tensor distributions) are ultimately required for engineering design./Fig. 16 NeuralDEM for real time simulations of industrial particulate flows (Alkin et al., 2025)For the present research (RO4), the Dynami-CAL architecture provides the primary inspiration for the physics-consistent GNN design, with NeuralDEM informing the eventual upscaling from particle-level to continuum-level predictions.Research gaps and positioning of the present studyThe foregoing review identifies the following critical research gaps:Gap 1. No existing framework for geomaterial constitutive modeling enforces both the first and second laws of thermodynamics as hard architectural constraints simultaneously, without any reliance on soft penalty terms.Gap 2. The transition from trained thermodynamically consistent neural networks to interpretable, symbolic constitutive expressions has not been demonstrated for geomaterials. The symbolic regression approaches of Flaschel et al. (2021) and Abdusalamov et al. (2023) were restricted to simple elastic and elastoplastic models without thermodynamic consistency.Gap 3. The validation of physics-driven neural constitutive models under complex cyclic loading conditions representative of offshore geotechnical scenarios—using actual laboratory data from standardized testing programs—has not been reported.Gap 4. Physics-consistent GNN simulators with embedded thermodynamic and contact mechanical constraints have not been applied to the discrete particle simulation of geomaterials.The present research programme is uniquely positioned to address all four gaps through a coherent, progressive research agenda.MethodologyOverall research frameworkThe research framework is built around a central principle: observable inputs + thermodynamic first principles → constitutive law. Accuracy validation: Integration of the discovered constitutive law along standard stress paths and comparison against both the t-PiNet predictions and the original MCC/DSS training data.Step 2. Physical consistency check: Verification that the discovered free energy satisfies convexity conditions, that the yield surface is closed and convex, and that the hardening law produces positive dissipation.Step 3. Cross-validation: Testing on held-out experimental data (e.g., triaxial tests at stress ratios not included in training) to confirm that the discovered expression generalizes correctly.Step 4. Dimensional analysis: Verification that the discovered expression is dimensionally consistent, using the Buckingham π theorem where applicable.RO3: Sim-to-Real adaptation and cyclic loading validation with NGI experimental dataFrom monotonic synthetic to cyclic real: A staged adaptation strategyRO3 serves as the primary sim-to-real validation stage for the continuum-scale framework. A key challenge must be recognized from the outset: the transition from RO1 to RO3 involves not only a shift in the data domain from synthetic to real data, but also a gap in constitutive capability from monotonic to cyclic behavior. The MCC model used to generate the RO1 training data is inherently unable to reproduce cyclic phenomena such as hysteresis loops, progressive pore pressure accumulation, and cyclic stiffness degradation, as it relies on isotropic hardening without any mechanism to capture load reversal memory. As a result, the RO1-trained t-PiNet has not been exposed to cyclic behavior during training, and this limitation cannot be overcome by thermodynamic constraints alone. While such constraints ensure physical admissibility, they cannot introduce constitutive information that is absent from the training data.To bridge this gap honestly and systematically, the adaptation strategy proceeds in four phases:Phase 1: Cyclic synthetic pre-training (bridging the capability gap). Before confronting real experimental data, the t-PiNet is re-trained or fine-tuned on cyclic synthetic data generated from cyclically capable constitutive models—specifically, a bounding surface model with kinematic hardening (Dafalias and Herrmann, 1986) or the SANISAND-MS model (Liu et al., 2019), calibrated to generic clay parameters. This phase introduces the fundamental cyclic phenomena into the model's training experience while remaining in the controlled synthetic environment where ground-truth is exact. The thermodynamic hard constraints are maintained throughout, ensuring that the cyclic extension remains physically admissible. This phase is methodologically critical: it separates the constitutive-capability gap (monotonic → cyclic, addressed by Phase 1) from the data-domain gap (synthetic → real, addressed by Phases 2~4).Phase 2: Zero-shot transfer assessment. The Phase-1-trained t-PiNet (now cyclically capable) is directly evaluated on NGI cyclic DSS data without any further retraining. This measures the "sim-to-real gap" in its pure form: how much the cyclic synthetic training environment differs from real Drammen clay behavior. Key metrics are compared against the target accuracy thresholds defined in Section 3.4.3.Phase 3 focuses on physics-constrained fine-tuning. If zero-shot transfer proves insufficient, which is expected due to remaining discrepancies between idealized bounding surface models and real clay behavior such as fabric anisotropy, rate dependence, and specimen variability, the model is fine-tuned using a subset of NGI training data. During this process, thermodynamic hard constraints are strictly preserved, including the stress-from-free-energy relationship and the enforcement of non-negative dissipation through softplus activation. This ensures that the model cannot adapt to real data by exploiting thermodynamically inconsistent shortcuts, such as reproducing hysteresis loops while violating energy balance. The fine-tuning uses a significantly lower learning rate to preserve the learned thermodynamic structure while adapting to soil-specific cyclic behavior./Fig. 18 Overview of the physics-constrained fine-tuning framework. research framework for a PhD programme that develops physics-driven deep learning methodologies for constitutive modeling and scientific discovery in geomechanics, positioned within the AI for Science (AI4S) paradigm.Chapter 1 introduced the research problem, the four research objectives (RO1–RO4), the expected contributions, and the research schedule. Chapter 2 provided a comprehensive literature review organized around each research objective, critically identifying the methodological limitations of classical phenomenological models, purely data-driven approaches, and existing physics-informed neural network applications in geomechanics—thereby establishing the research gaps that motivate this work. Chapter 3 detailed the proposed methodology for each sub-study, including the t-PiNet architecture with thermodynamic hard constraints (RO1), the symbolic regression pipeline for constitutive knowledge discovery (RO2), the adaptation strategy validated against NGI cyclic experimental data (RO3), and the physics-consistent GNN simulator for discrete particulate systems (RO4). Chapter 4 presented the current progress on RO1, demonstrating that the t-PiNet framework achieves accurate stress–strain predictions across multiple over-consolidation ratios, guarantees thermodynamic admissibility at every time step, and—most notably—generalizes to unseen confining pressures without retraining, providing early evidence that thermodynamic hard constraints can serve as effective inductive biases for out-of-distribution generalization.In summary, this research constitutes a deeply interdisciplinary PhD programme at the intersection of computational mechanics, artificial intelligence, and geotechnical engineering. It aims to demonstrate that embedding physical first principles as hard architectural constraints into machine learning models can simultaneously achieve what no single existing paradigm offers alone: thermodynamic admissibility, data-driven flexibility, human-interpretable knowledge extraction, and robust transferability from synthetic to experimental settings. The research is on track, with RO1 completed by end of Year 1, positioning the programme for timely progression through the remaining objectives in collaboration with the Norwegian Geotechnical Institute.ReferencesAbdusalamov, R., Hillgärtner, M., Itskov, M., 2023.
